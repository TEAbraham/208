Point and interval estimates

Statistical estimation methods provide estimates for the value of unknown population parameters based on a sample of data. The two statistical estimation methods are point estimates and interval estimates. A point estimate is a single value estimate for an unknown parameter. Ex: A sample of \(15\) employees have a mean of \(38.4\) hours worked weekly which is a point estimate for the population mean hours worked weekly for all employees at the company. An interval estimate is a range of possible values for the parameter being estimated. Ex: Based on the sample of \(15\) employees, a \(95\%\) confidence interval for the population mean hours worked weekly is \((35.2,41.6)\) .

Example 5.1.1: How millennials acquire news.

A survey by the Media Insight Project asked a random sample of \(1,045\) American adults between the ages of \(18\) and \(34\) questions about technology used when acquiring news and frequency. The results for a few of the survey questions are given in the table below.

Percentage of millennials who: \(\%\) \(95\%\)Confidence interval
Say keeping up with the news is at least somewhat important \(85\%\) \((81.2,88.8)\)
Get news daily \(69\%\) \((65.2,72.8)\)
Regularly follow five or more “hard” news topics \(45\%\) \((41.2,48.8)\)
Usually see diverse opinions through social media \(86\%\) \((82.2,89.8)\)
Pay for at least one news-specific service, app, or digital subscription \(40\%\) \((36.2,43.8)\)
  1. What is the point estimate for the percent of millennials who get their news daily?
  2. What is the \(95\%\) confidence interval for the percent of millennials who get their news daily?
  3. What is the interpretation of this interval estimate?

Solution

  1. \(69\%\)
  2. \((65.2,72.8)\)
  3. Based on the sample data collected, the true percentage of all millennials who get news daily is believed to be between \(65.2\%\) and \(72.8\%\) with \(95\%\) confidence.

Accuracy and precision

A good estimation method gives estimates that are both accurate and precise. Accuracy is measured in terms of bias, or how close on average the estimate is to the population parameter being estimated. Numerically, bias is the distance between the mean of the sampling distribution and the population parameter being estimated. Precision is how variable estimates are to one another if the estimation process is repeated. Precision is measured in terms of the standard error of the sampling distribution.

An interval estimate is considered accurate if the interval contains the true population parameter, and an interval estimate’s precision refers to the width of the interval.

5.1.1: Accuracy and precision.

Captions:

  1. Accuracy is a measure of closeness to the true value of a parameter. Precision is a measure of closeness to the average value of the estimates.
  2. Estimates that are neither accurate nor precise are neither close to the true value nor tightly clustered.
  3. Estimates that are accurate but not precise are loosely spread out around the true value.
  4. Estimates that are precise but not accurate are tightly clustered around an incorrect value.
  5. Estimates that are both accurate and precise are tightly clustered around the true value.

Confidence intervals

A confidence interval is an interval estimate constructed such that the probability the method produces an interval that contains the population parameter is given. The percentage of times the method produces a confidence interval containing the parameter is called the confidence level, which is denoted by \(c\) . Common values for \(c\) are \(90\%\) , \(95\%\) , and \(98\%\) .

A confidence interval is constructed from the sample statistic and margin of error. The margin of error, denoted by \(m\) , is a measure of the estimate’s precision and is constructed from a multiplier and the standard error of the statistic’s sampling distribution. The multiplier, or critical value, for a \(c\%\) confidence interval is the number of standard deviations about the mean of the sampling distribution that would capture \(c\%\) of the distribution.

Numerically: \[m = (mulitplier)(standard error)\]

Therefore: \[estimate\pm m = estimate\pm(mulitplier)(standard error)\]

Confidence intervals are reported either by giving the estimate and margin or error, \(estimate\pm m\) , or by calculating and reporting the lower and upper bounds of the interval, \((estimate - m, estimate + m)\) .

5.1.2: A \(95\%\) confidence interval for a population parameter.

Captions: 1. Suppose the true value of the population parameter is \(4\) . 2. A sample is drawn from the population. The sample statistic is \(3.5\) , so \(3.5\) is a point estimate for the population parameter. 3. The margin of error is \(\pm2.5\) at a \(95\%\) confidence level. Thus, the constructed \(95\%\) confidence interval is \((3.5 - 2.5, 3.5 + 2.5) = (1,6)\) . 4. The first sample statistic is one possible outcome. The process of drawing a sample from the population and constructing a \(95\%\) confidence interval can be repeated. 5. If samples are drawn repeatedly, the percentage of constructed \(95\%\) confidence intervals containing the true parameter is \(95\%\) , or \(19\) of \(20\) intervals are expected to contain the true population parameter.

5.2 Confidence intervals for population means

\(z\)-confidence intervals

Choosing an appropriate estimator for the population mean \(\mu\) depends on what is known about the population. Although the population mean is usually unknown, the confidence interval that quantifies the range within which the true population mean lies can be calculated by sampling the population. Ex: A \(95\%\) confidence interval means that the interval calculated has a \(95\%\) probability of containing the population mean. If such a population is sampled \(20\) times, \(19\) of the \(20\) calculated intervals are expected to contain the population mean.

5.2.1: The \(z\) -confidence interval.

Captions: 1. A confidence interval for the population mean of a normal distribution is calculated from the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) , standard deviation \(\sigma\) , and number of samples \(n\) . 2. The margin of error \(m\) for the population mean is equal to \(z^*\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\) . 3. The confidence level \(c\) is the area under the normal distribution curve in the interval \((\bar{x} - m, \bar{x} + m)\) . 4. The significance level \(\alpha\) is the area under the curve outside the confidence interval.

The critical values \(z^*\) for common confidence levels are shown in the following table.

Table 5.2.1: Critical values for common confidence levels.

Confidence level Critical value
\(c=0.90\) \(z^*=1.645\)
\(c=0.95\) \(z^*=1.960\)
\(c=0.99\) \(z^*=2.576\)

Example 5.2.1: Confidence interval for the mean final grade.

Suppose the mean final grade is estimated for all introductory statistics classes taught at a particular college. The population standard deviation is \(4\) . The final grades for a randomly selected statistics class with \(9\) people are: \(76\) , \(80\) , \(82\) , \(83\) , \(83\) , \(85\) , \(85\) , \(87\) , and \(88\) . Find the \(95\%\) confidence interval for the mean final grade.

Solution

The sample mean is \[\bar{x} = \frac{76+80+82+83+83+85+85+87+88}{9} = 83.222\] At the \(95\%\) confidence level, \(z^* = 1.960\) , so the margin of error is \[m = z^*\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\] \[= 1.960*\frac{4}{\sqrt{9}}\] \[\approx 2.613\] Thus, the \(95\%\) confidence interval is \((83.222 - 2.613, 83.222 + 2.613) = (80.609, 85.835)\) .

The population mean final grade is generally unknown. The obtained interval means that a \(95\%\) confidence exists that \((80.609, 85.835)\) contains the true population mean final grade.

Margin of error and sample size for means when \(\sigma\) is known

The width of the confidence interval is twice the margin of error. Recall that the margin of error depends on the confidence level and the standard error. Thus, given a confidence level, the width of the confidence interval changes by changing the standard error. Increasing the sample size decreases the standard error. Similarly, decreasing the sample size increases the standard error. The size of the sample needed to guarantee a confidence interval with a specified margin of error is given by the formula \[n = (\frac{z^*\sigma}{m})^2\] To help guarantee a confidence interval with a margin of error no greater than desired, the result of the above equation is rounded up to the nearest whole number sample size.

Example 5.2.2: Microbeads in a water reservoir.

Scientists study the amount of microbeads (in \(\mu\) g/liter) in a water reservoir. The scientists need to know how many water samples should be taken to be \(95\%\) confident that their estimate \(\bar{x}\) differs from the actual value \(\mu\) by at most \(0.8 \mu\) g/liter given a population standard deviation of \(\sigma = 2.5 \mu\) g/liter. How many water samples should the scientists take?

Solution

The margin of error is \(m = 0.8\) and the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 2.5\) . Since the confidence level is \(95\%\) , the critical value is \(z^* = 1.960\) . Using the formula above,

\[n = (\frac{z^*\sigma}{m})^2 = (\frac{1.960 * 2.5}{0.8})^2 \approx 37.516\] Thus, the scientists should take at least \(38\) water samples.

The value obtained for \(n\) should always be rounded up, instead of simply rounded to the nearest integer. Using \(37\) water samples, for example, would not be sufficient to guarantee a confidence interval with a specific width.

\(t\)-confidence intervals

The confidence interval for a population mean is defined as

\[(\bar{x} - z^* \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar{x} + z^* \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}})\]

In practice, however, the population standard deviation is rarely known. Thus, confidence intervals using the \(t\) -distribution are more useful because the sample standard deviation can always be computed.

The \(t\) -confidence interval is given by

\[(\bar{x} - t^* \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar{x} + t^* \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}})\]

where \(t^*\) is the critical value that depends on the degrees of freedom and significance level. The values for common significance levels given selected degrees of freedom are shown in the table below.

Table 5.2.2: Critical values \(t^*\) for selected degrees of freedom \(df\) and significance level \(\alpha\).

\(\alpha=0.1\) \(\alpha=0.05\) \(\alpha=0.01\)
\(df=5\) \(2.015\) \(2.571\) \(4.032\)
\(df=10\) \(1.812\) \(2.228\) \(3.169\)
\(df=15\) \(1.753\) \(2.131\) \(2.947\)
\(df=24\) \(1.711\) \(2.064\) \(2.797\)

Example 5.2.3: Circumference of basketballs

The mean circumference of basketballs produced in a manufacturing facility is supposed to be \(29\) inches. A random sample of \(25\) basketballs has a mean of \(29.1\) inches with a sample standard deviation of \(0.217\) inches. Find the confidence interval at the \(\alpha = 0.01\) significance level.

Solution

At a significance level of \(\alpha = 0.01\) , the confidence level is \(99\%\) . The critical value that corresponds to a \(99\%\) confidence level and degrees of freedom \(df = 25 - 1 = 24\) is \(t^* = 2.797\) from the table above. Thus, the margin of error is

\[m = t^* \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = 2.797\frac{0.217}{\sqrt{25}} \approx 0.121\]

Thus, the \(99\%\) confidence interval is

\[(\bar{x} - m, \bar{x} + m) = (29.1 - 0.121, 1 + 0.121) = (28.979, 29.221)\]

The obtained confidence interval suggests that a \(99\%\) confidence exists that the true population mean circumference of basketballs produced in the facility is between \(28.979\) and \(29.221\) inches.

Margin of error and sample size for means when \(\sigma\) is unknown

The \(z\) -distribution is commonly used to estimate the necessary sample size for a survey. Since the population standard deviation is generally unknown, the \(t\) -distribution should be used instead. For a situation with an unknown population standard deviation, the sample standard deviation should be used. Thus, \[n = (\frac{t^*s}{m})^2\] where \(t^*\) is the \(t\) critical value at a specified confidence level, \(s\) is the sample standard deviation based on a preliminary study, and \(m\) is the margin of error.

However, \(t^*\) depends on the degrees of freedom \(n - 1\) , which is also in terms of \(n\) . To find \(t^*\) , \(z^*\) is used to find a preliminary sample size \(n^*\) . That is, \[n^* = (\frac{z^*s}{m})^2\] Then \(n\) can be calculated using \[n = (\frac{t^*s}{m})^2\] where \(t^*\) is found using \(df = n^* - 1\) . To help guarantee a confidence interval with a margin of error no greater than desired, the result of the above equation is rounded up to the nearest whole number sample size.

Example 5.2.4: Quality control.

A quality control department in a company wants to estimate the average length of a part to within a margin of error \(\pm0.35\) cm at \(95\%\) confidence interval. A preliminary pilot study found that the sample standard deviation of the length of parts is \(2.50\) cm. How many parts should the department use?

Solution

The formula above uses \(t^*\) , and thus a preliminary sample size \(n^*\) is needed. To find \(n^*\) , \(z^*\) is used in the formula instead of \(t^*\) . Since \(z^* = 1.960\) at the \(95\%\) confidence level, \[n^* = (\frac{z^*s}{m})^2 = (\frac{1.960 * 2.50}{0.35})^2 = 196\] The critical \(t\) value that corresponds to \(df = n^* - 1 = 196 - 1 = 195\) at the \(95\%\) confidence level is \(t^* = 1.972\) . For a \(95\%\) confidence interval, the percentile of the \(t\) -distribution to find the critical value is \(1 - \frac{1-0.95}{2}=0.975\) . The critical value can be found using \(t^* = invT(0.975, 195) = 1.972\) . Thus, \[n^* = (\frac{t^*s}{m})^2 = (\frac{1.972 * 2.50}{0.35})^2 \approx 198.407\] Rounding this result up to the nearest whole number, the quality control department should use a sample size of \(199\) parts.

If the sample size was instead rounded down to 198, then the expected margin of error is \(1.972 * \frac{2.50}{\sqrt{198}} \approx 0.3504\) , which is slightly higher than the desired 0.35. Rounding the sample size up to 199 gives an expected margin of error of \(1.972 * \frac{2.50}{\sqrt{199}} \approx 0.3495\) .

Confidence interval for population proportions

Constructing a confidence interval for a population proportion is similar to constructing a confidence interval for population means. Suppose the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) and the number of samples \(n\) are known from sampling the population. The margin of error \(m\) is given by \(m = z^*\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\) , where \(z^*\) is the critical value corresponding to the desired confidence level \(c\) . The confidence interval for the population proportion \(p\) is given by \((\hat{p} - m, \hat{p} + m)\) .

Example 5.3.1: Confidence interval for the proportion.

In a survey of \(1200\) randomly selected registered voters in a particular city, \(348\) people are in favor of banning public smoking. State and interpret the \(95\%\) confidence interval for the population proportion of voters in favor of banning public smoking.

Solution

The sample proportion is \[\hat{p} = \frac{348}{1200} = 0.29\] At the \(95\%\) confidence level, the margin of error is \[m = z^*\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = 1.960\sqrt{\frac{0.29(1-0.29)}{1200}} \approx 0.026\] Thus, the \(95\%\) confidence interval is \[(\hat{p} - m, \hat{p} + m) = (0.29 - 0.026, 0.29 + 0.026) = (0.264, 0.316)\] The \(95\%\) confidence level suggests that if the same estimator was used to construct the interval, then the true population parameter would be within the interval \(95\%\) of the time. The obtained confidence interval suggests a \(95\%\) confidence exists that the true proportion of voters who favor banning smoking is between \(26.4\%\) and \(31.6\%\) .

Margin of error and sample size for proportions

Finding the necessary sample size given a confidence level and margin of error is similar to finding the sample size for a confidence interval for population means. The size of the sample needed to guarantee a confidence interval with a specified margin of error is given by the formula \[n = (\frac{z^*}{m})^2p(1-p)\] where \(z^*\) is the critical value at a specified confidence level, \(p\) is the proportion, and \(m\) is the margin of error. To help guarantee a confidence interval with a margin of error no greater than desired, the result of the above equation is rounded up to the nearest whole number sample size.

However, \(p\) also depends on \(n\) . Two possible options exist:

  1. A preliminary value for \(p\) can be used based on a previous study.
  2. The worst case scenario with \(p = 0.50\) can be used, which provides the largest sample size needed to satisfy the required margin of error at a specified confidence level.

Example 5.3.2: Six months after Brexit.

Brexit is a popular term for the successful referendum on June 2016 for the withdrawal of the United Kingdom from the European Union. Six months later, a poll conducted by CNN and ComRes resulted in \(47\%\) of the respondents voting to leave the European Union had the referendum been held that day. If the margin of error is \(\pm 2.17\) percentage points, find the sample size needed for a \(90\%\) confidence interval if

  1. \(p = 0.47\) is used
  2. no previous estimate is given

Solution

  1. The margin of error is \(m = 0.0217\) . Since a previous estimate is known, the sample proportion is \(p=0.47\) . Since the confidence level is \(90\%\) , the critical value is \(z^* = 1.645\) . Using the formula above, \[n = (\frac{z^*}{m})^2p(1-p) = (\frac{1.645}{0.0217})^20.47(1-0.47) \approx 1431.483\] Rounding this result up to the nearest whole number, the pollsters sampled at least \(1432\) voting aged adults.

  2. When no previous estimate is known, \(p = 0.5\) is used. Using the same formula gives, \[n = (\frac{z^*}{m})^2p(1-p) = (\frac{1.645}{0.0217})^20.50(1-0.50) \approx 1436.655\] Thus, the pollsters would have sampled \(1437\) voting aged adults if no previous estimate is given in the news story.
