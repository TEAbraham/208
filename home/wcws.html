ü•é Case Study: Do Teams with Higher Batting Averages Perform Better in the NCAA Softball Tournament?

1. Exploring One-Variable Data

  • Focus: Team Batting Average (BA) from hitting_team.csv
  • What you do: Make a histogram and boxplot of team batting averages.
  • Insight: See how batting performance is distributed‚Äîright-skewed? symmetric? outliers?

2. Exploring Two-Variable Data

  • Focus: Relationship between team batting average and team ERA (earned run average).
  • What you do: Merge hitting_team with pitching_team and create a scatterplot of BA vs. ERA.
  • Insight: Investigate if teams that hit well also pitch well (or if they trade off).

3. Collecting Data

  • Focus: Understanding the source
  • What you do: Note that these stats are season aggregates, not random samples. Discuss biases (e.g., strength of schedule).
  • Insight: Acknowledge that without random sampling, generalization to all teams must be cautious.

4. Probability, Random Variables, and Distributions

  • Focus: Simulating game outcomes
  • What you do: Use a simplified model ‚Äî simulate outcomes between high-BA vs. low-BA teams using win probability logic.
  • Insight: Begin modeling how likely it is a high-BA team would outperform just by chance.

5. Sampling Distributions

  • Focus: Sampling variability of team BA
  • What you do: Bootstrap resample from the list of teams to simulate the sampling distribution of the mean team BA.
  • Insight: Understand margin of error if you were estimating average D1 team BA.

6. Confidence Intervals

  • Focus: Estimate average team BA in the tournament bracket
  • What you do: From the 64 teams in the bracket, construct a 95% confidence interval for their mean BA.
  • Insight: See if tournament teams hit better than the national average.

7. Hypothesis Testing

  • Focus: Test: ‚ÄúDo tournament teams have significantly higher BA than non-tournament teams?‚Äù
  • What you do: Perform a one-sample t-test using national team BA as a baseline.
  • Insight: Determine statistical significance of elite hitting performance.

8. Inference for Categorical Data: Chi-Square

  • Focus: Classify teams into high, medium, and low BA tiers
  • What you do: Run a chi-square test on bracket progression vs. BA tier (e.g., Do high-BA teams make it to Super Regionals more?)
  • Insight: Test independence between categorical performance and outcome.

9. Inference for Quantitative Data: Means

  • Focus: Compare ERA or BA between teams that advanced past Regionals vs. those that didn‚Äôt.
  • What you do: Use a two-sample t-test.
  • Insight: See if pitching or hitting significantly differentiates winning teams.

# Categorize bracket teams into BA and ERA tiers
def tiered_column(series, quantiles=(0.33, 0.66), labels=("Low", "Mid", "High")):
    return pd.qcut(series, q=[0, quantiles[0], quantiles[1], 1], labels=labels)

# Merge hitting and pitching team data on cleaned team names
merged = pd.merge(
    hitting_team[["Team_clean", "BA"]],
    pitching_team[["Team_clean", "ERA"]],
    on="Team_clean",
    how="inner"
)

# Add tournament status
merged["In_Tournament"] = merged["Team_clean"].isin(
    [team.replace(".", "").replace("St", "State").replace("Fla", "Florida").strip() for team in bracket_teams]
)

# Assign BA and ERA tiers
merged["BA_Tier"] = tiered_column(merged["BA"])
merged["ERA_Tier"] = tiered_column(merged["ERA"], labels=["High", "Mid", "Low"])  # Lower ERA is better

# Create contingency tables
ba_table = pd.crosstab(merged["BA_Tier"], merged["In_Tournament"])
era_table = pd.crosstab(merged["ERA_Tier"], merged["In_Tournament"])

# Chi-square tests
chi2_ba, p_ba, _, _ = stats.chi2_contingency(ba_table)
chi2_era, p_era, _, _ = stats.chi2_contingency(era_table)

# Return results
{

}

Now let‚Äôs build a logistic regression model using both BA and ERA to predict tournament inclusion.

Linear vs Logistic Regression

Linear Regression Logistic Regression
Used to predict the continuous dependent variable using a given set of independent variables. Used to predict the categorical dependent variable using a given set of independent variables.
The outputs produced must be a continuous value, such as price and age. The outputs produced must be Categorical values such as 0 or 1, Yes or No.
The relationship between the dependent variable and independent variable must be linear. The relationship DOES NOT need to be linear between the dependent and independent variables.
Used for solving Regression problems. Used for solving Classification problems.
We are finding and using the line of best fit to help us easily predict outputs. We are using the S-curve (Sigmoid) to help us classify predicted outputs.
Least square estimation method is used for the estimation of accuracy. Maximum likelihood estimation method is used for the estimation of accuracy.
There is a possibility of collinearity between the independent variables. There should not be any collinearity between the independent variable.

üßÆ How Each Was Calculated ‚Äî Step-by-Step:

  1. Matchup Normalization: We matched team names from the bracket and stat files using standardized lowercase names (e.g., "southeasternla").

  2. Look Up Stats: For each team, we extracted:

    • Batting Average (BA)
    • Earned Run Average (ERA)

  3. Prediction via Logistic Model: Using this model:

    \[ \text{logit}(p) = -7.16 + 34.73 \cdot \text{BA} - 1.28 \cdot \text{ERA} \]

    we calculated the win probability for both teams.

  4. Upset Probability: We define this as:

    \[ \text{Upset Probability} = P(\text{Underdog Wins}) - P(\text{Favorite Wins}) \]

    If this number is positive and large, the underdog is predicted to be stronger than the favorite.
